# Indonesian Curriculum: A1 to A2 (Beginner)

**Target**: English-speaking learners (expats, travelers, digital nomads in Indonesia/Bali)
**CEFR Range**: A1 (breakthrough) through A2 (waystage)
**Total Units**: 10 units, 50 scenes
**Total Vocabulary**: ~580 words (frequency-ranked, drawn from subtitle corpora and textbook lists)
**Estimated Learning Time**: 25-35 hours (at ~30 min per scene)

---

## Curriculum Design Principles

1. **Frequency-first vocabulary**: Words are selected from Indonesian frequency lists (subtitle corpora, Wikipedia frequency analysis) and ranked by real-world communicative utility. Function words (yang, dan, di, untuk) are introduced through exposure in phrases rather than taught as standalone vocabulary items.

2. **Scene-based contextual learning**: Each scene is a real-world situation a learner might encounter in Indonesia. Vocabulary and grammar are always taught in context, never in isolation.

3. **Spiral grammar**: Grammar points are introduced receptively first (learners encounter them in dialogues), then practiced productively in later scenes. Affixes are introduced gradually, starting with frozen/lexicalized forms before teaching productive rules.

4. **Register awareness**: Formal Indonesian (bahasa baku) is taught first, with informal variants (bahasa gaul) introduced progressively starting in Unit 2. The curriculum flags which register each expression belongs to.

5. **Cultural embedding**: Each unit includes cultural notes relevant to the situations, helping learners navigate not just language but social norms.

6. **Existing content integration**: 7 existing scenes (Selamat Pagi, Nama Saya, Siapa Itu, Saya Mau, Enak Sekali, Berapa Harganya, Di Mana) are incorporated into Units 1-4 and Unit 6 with their exact titles preserved.

## Grammar Progression (Overview)

| Units | Grammar Focus |
|-------|--------------|
| 1-2 | SVO word order, equative sentences (no copula), question words (apa, siapa, di mana), basic negation (tidak), possessives (saya, Anda), demonstratives (ini, itu) |
| 3-4 | More question words (berapa, kapan, mengapa, bagaimana), negation (tidak vs. bukan), adjective placement (noun + adj), intensifiers (sekali, sangat), numbers 1-1000, basic classifiers (buah, orang, ekor) |
| 5-6 | me- prefix (active verbs), ber- prefix (intransitive verbs), possessive -nya, comparatives (lebih ... dari), time expressions, modal verbs (bisa, harus, boleh, mau, ingin) |
| 7-8 | di- prefix (passive voice), -kan suffix (causative), ter- prefix (superlatives, accidental), -an suffix (nominalization), time conjunctions (sebelum, sesudah, ketika) |
| 9-10 | pe-/-an (agent/process nouns), ke-/-an (abstract nouns), me-/-kan (compound transitive), relative clauses (yang), conditional (kalau/jika), expressing opinions |

### Grammar Points — Unit 1

1. **Equative sentences (no copula)**: Indonesian has no "to be." "Saya Adi" = "I am Adi." "Ini kamar" = "This is a room."
2. **SVO word order**: Subject-Verb-Object. "Saya mau kamar" = "I want a room."
3. **Question words — apa, di mana, berapa**: Placed where the answer would go. "Nama Anda apa?" or "Apa nama Anda?"
4. **Basic negation — tidak**: Placed before verbs and adjectives. "Tidak besar" = "not big."
5. **Possessives — saya, Anda**: Possessor follows the noun. "Nama saya" = "my name" (lit. "name I").
6. **Demonstratives — ini, itu**: "Ini" (this, proximal), "itu" (that, distal). Can function as subjects: "Ini kamar saya" = "This is my room."
7. **ada**: Existential verb meaning "there is" or "to have." "Ada kamar?" = "Is there a room?" / "Do you have a room?"

### Affix Notes — Unit 1

- **se-** in "selamat": Frozen form. Do not teach se- as a productive prefix yet.
- **ber-** in "bertemu": Encountered as a set vocabulary item. Note to learners: "This word starts with ber- which you will learn about later."
- **terima kasih**: Teach as a single phrase. "Terima" (receive) + "kasih" (give/love) — etymology is interesting but the phrase is a fixed unit.

### Register Notes — Unit 1

- Unit 1 uses **formal register** (bahasa baku) throughout.
- "Anda" (formal you) is used exclusively. "Kamu" introduced in Unit 2.
- "Saya" (formal I) is used exclusively. "Aku" mentioned as existing but not practiced.

### Cultural Notes — Unit 1

- **Time-of-day greetings**: Indonesians greet by time of day, not just "hello." Pagi (morning, ~6-10am), siang (midday, ~11am-3pm), sore (late afternoon, ~3-6pm), malam (evening/night, ~6pm+).
- **Handshake culture**: Light handshake, sometimes followed by touching your own chest. No bone-crushing grips.
- **"Dari mana?"**: This is the most common small-talk question. Indonesians are genuinely curious about where foreigners are from.
- **Airport arrival**: Indonesia requires visa on arrival (VOA) for most nationalities. "Visa on arrival" signs are in English, but being able to say basic phrases to immigration officers is appreciated.

### Grammar Points — Unit 2

1. **Prepositions of place**: di (in/at), ke (to, direction), dari (from). "Di" for static location, "ke" for movement toward, "dari" for origin/movement away.
2. **Compound prepositions**: di depan (in front of), di belakang (behind), di samping (beside), di atas (above), di bawah (below), di antara (between).
3. **Question words — kapan, bagaimana**: "Kapan?" (when?), introduced in context of transport schedules.
4. **Reduplication**: First exposure to doubled words — "pelan-pelan" (slowly), "hati-hati" (careful), "jalan-jalan" (stroll/walk around). Teach as vocabulary, note the pattern.
5. **sudah + verb**: Indicates completed action. "Sudah sampai" = "Already arrived." Contrast with "belum" (not yet).
6. **Numbers 1-10 and tens**: satu, dua, tiga, empat, lima, enam, tujuh, delapan, sembilan, sepuluh. Tens formed with X + puluh (dua puluh = 20).

### Affix Notes — Unit 2

- **ber-** in "berhenti" (stop), "berjalan" (walk): Encountered receptively. Note: "ber- makes verbs that describe a state or activity without a direct object."
- **se-** in "sepuluh" (ten), "setengah" (half), "sebentar" (a moment): Pattern emerging — se- = "one" or "a."
- **Reduplication**: "pelan-pelan," "hati-hati," "jalan-jalan" — explain that doubling can intensify, soften, or change meaning.

### Register Notes — Unit 2

- **Introduce kamu and aku**: The taxi driver or ojek driver uses "kamu" (informal you) and "aku" (informal I). Explain: kamu/aku = friends, peers, casual; Anda/saya = formal, strangers, service.
- **Mas/Mbak**: Introduce "Mas" (brother, for young men) and "Mbak" (sister, for young women) as casual address forms used with drivers, shopkeepers, waiters.
- **Pak/Bu**: "Pak" (sir/Mr.) and "Bu" (ma'am/Mrs.) for more formal or older people.

### Cultural Notes — Unit 2

- **Ojek culture**: Grab and Gojek are ubiquitous in Indonesian cities. Ordering by app, but speaking to the driver is expected.
- **Bargaining for taxis**: Non-metered taxis (especially in Bali) require price negotiation. Always agree on price before getting in.
- **Traffic**: Indonesian traffic is chaotic by Western standards. "Pelan-pelan" and "hati-hati" are genuinely useful phrases.
- **Motorbike etiquette**: Helmets required by law. Sit sideways if wearing a skirt (for women). Hold the grab bar, not the driver.

### Grammar Points — Unit 3

1. **Intensifiers — sekali vs. sangat**: "Sekali" follows the adjective: "enak sekali" (very delicious). "Sangat" precedes: "sangat enak." Both mean "very" but sekali is more colloquial.
2. **terlalu (too)**: Precedes adjective. "Terlalu mahal" = too expensive. "Terlalu pedas" = too spicy.
3. **Suka + noun/verb**: "Saya suka kopi" (I like coffee). "Saya suka makan" (I like eating). Negated: "Saya tidak suka pedas."
4. **Polite requests — minta vs. mau**: "Minta" is more polite than "mau." "Saya minta air putih" (I'd like water, please) vs. "Saya mau air putih" (I want water).
5. **Modal verb — bisa**: "Bisa" = can/able. "Bisa kurang?" (Can you lower it?). "Bisa bayar pakai kartu?" (Can I pay by card?).
6. **Adjective placement**: Adjectives follow nouns. "Air panas" (hot water), "es teh manis" (sweet iced tea), "nasi goreng pedas" (spicy fried rice).

### Affix Notes — Unit 3

- **me-** encountered receptively: "memesan" (to order, formal written form of "pesan"), "memilih" (to choose). Not yet taught as productive rule — learners just see these in menu descriptions or formal speech.
- **ter-** in "terlalu": Frozen form. Learners treat "terlalu" as a single word meaning "too (much)."

### Register Notes — Unit 3

- **Warung vs. restoran**: Warung language is casual. At a warung, "Mau apa?" (Want what?) is normal. At a restoran, "Mau pesan apa, Pak?" is more common.
- **"Mas" / "Mbak"** for calling waitstaff: "Mas, minta bon!" (Brother, the bill please!).
- **Nggak**: Informal variant of "tidak." "Nggak mau" = "Don't want." Introduce receptively — learners will hear this everywhere.

### Cultural Notes — Unit 3

- **Warung culture**: Small family-run food stalls. Often the best and cheapest food. Point-and-choose is acceptable.
- **Sambal**: Every meal comes with sambal (chili paste). Always ask "Ada sambal?" — it is usually available.
- **Eating with hands**: Traditional eating uses the right hand. Left hand is considered unclean. Spoons and forks (not knives) are standard utensils.
- **"Sudah makan?"**: Literally "Have you eaten?" — a common greeting showing care, not literally asking about food.
- **Kopi Bali**: Balinese coffee is typically unfiltered (grounds settle at the bottom). "Kopi tubruk" = this style.

### Grammar Points — Unit 4

1. **-nya suffix (the/its)**: The definite article and third-person possessive. "Harga" = price, "harganya" = the price / its price. "Warna" = color, "warnanya" = the color / its color. This is the most important suffix in Indonesian.
2. **Numbers 100-1,000,000**: seratus (100), dua ratus (200), seribu (1,000), sepuluh ribu (10,000), seratus ribu (100,000), satu juta (1,000,000). Pattern: se- = one for ratus, ribu.
3. **Classifiers**: buah (general objects, fruit), orang (people), ekor (animals). "Dua buah mangga" = two mangoes. "Tiga orang" = three people. "Lima ekor ayam" = five chickens.
4. **yang (relativizer)**: "Yang mana?" = Which one? "Yang merah" = The red one. "Ada yang lebih murah?" = Is there a cheaper one?
5. **Comparative — lebih + adjective + dari**: "Lebih murah dari itu" = Cheaper than that. Preview only — full treatment in Unit 6.
6. **ada yang...?**: "Ada yang lebih besar?" = Is there a bigger one? Pattern for shopping.

### Affix Notes — Unit 4

- **-nya**: PRODUCTIVE. Teach explicitly. Attaches to nouns ("harganya", "warnanya", "ukurannya") as "the [noun]" or "its [noun]". Also attaches to verbs in some patterns (later units).
- **se-** with numbers: PRODUCTIVE. "Seratus" (100), "seribu" (1,000). Pattern: se- = "one" before ratus, ribu, puluh.
- **ke-** in "kembalian": Receptive only. "Kembali" (return) -> "kembalian" (change/remainder). Note the pattern but don't teach ke-...-an yet.

### Register Notes — Unit 4

- **Bargaining language is inherently informal**: Even if using "saya/Anda," the transaction is casual. "Bisa kurang, Mas?" is the norm.
- **"Berapa?" alone**: In casual speech, just "Berapa?" without "harganya" is common. "Ini berapa?" = "How much is this?"
- **Number shortcuts**: "Seratus ribu" often shortened to "seratus" in market contexts where "ribu" is assumed. "Dua ratus" could mean 200 or 200,000 depending on context.

### Cultural Notes — Unit 4

- **Rupiah denominations**: Bills come in 1,000, 2,000, 5,000, 10,000, 20,000, 50,000, 100,000. No coins in practice. Everything is in thousands.
- **Bargaining etiquette**: Expected at traditional markets, not at malls or minimarkets. Start at 50-60% of asking price. Walk away slowly to get the best price. Always smile.
- **"Harga turis" vs. "harga lokal"**: Tourist price vs. local price. Being able to speak Indonesian often gets you closer to the local price.
- **Minimarket culture**: Indomaret and Alfamart are everywhere. Fixed prices, air conditioning, modern convenience. Say "Pakai kantong?" (Use a bag?) as bags cost extra.

### Grammar Points — Unit 5

1. **ber- prefix (intransitive verbs)**: PRODUCTIVE RULE. ber- + root = intransitive verb (no direct object). "Kerja" (work) -> "bekerja" (to work). "Ajar" (teach) -> "belajar" (to study). "Jalan" (road) -> "berjalan" (to walk). Note: sometimes ber- becomes be- (belajar) or ber- drops the r (berjalan is regular).
2. **Modal verbs**: harus (must), boleh (may/allowed), bisa (can), mau (want), ingin (want, more formal). Modal + verb: "Harus bersih" (must be clean). "Boleh masuk?" (May I enter?).
3. **Time expressions**: setiap hari (every day), setiap minggu (every week), setiap bulan (every month). kadang-kadang (sometimes), sering (often), jarang (rarely), selalu (always), biasanya (usually).
4. **sedang + verb**: Progressive/continuous aspect. "Sedang makan" = eating (right now). "Sedang hujan" = it's raining.
5. **dulu (first/beforehand)**: Placed after the verb. "Mandi dulu" = shower first. "Makan dulu" = eat first. Very common in daily Indonesian.
6. **Reduplication patterns**: "bersih-bersih" (cleaning, general cleaning up), "kadang-kadang" (sometimes), "buah-buahan" (fruits in general). Three patterns: intensity, plurality/generalization, habitual action.

### Affix Notes — Unit 5

- **ber-**: PRODUCTIVE. Teach the rule: ber- + root word = "to have / to do [something]" without a direct object.
  - bekerja (to work) = ber- + kerja
  - belajar (to study) = ber- + ajar (note: r drops when root starts with certain consonants)
  - bermain (to play) = ber- + main
  - berjalan (to walk) = ber- + jalan
- **-an** in "pakaian" (clothing), "sayuran" (vegetables), "buah-buahan" (fruits): Preview of nominalization suffix. "Pakai" (wear) -> "pakaian" (clothing). Explain pattern but don't require production yet.
- **ke-...-an** in "kebutuhan" (needs): Receptive exposure only. Note the pattern for Unit 10.
- **-nya** in "biasanya" (usually): Extending -nya beyond nouns. "Biasa" (usual) + "-nya" = "usually." Expanding understanding of -nya.

### Register Notes — Unit 5

- **Informal contractions**: "Nggak" for "tidak." "Udah" for "sudah." "Gimana" for "bagaimana." Introduce receptively — learners need to understand these.
- **Kos/kosan**: Informal housing terminology. "Kos" or "kosan" = boarding house room, extremely common for students and young workers.
- **"Ngapain?"**: Informal for "Sedang apa?" (What are you doing?). Very common in casual speech.

### Cultural Notes — Unit 5

- **Bali housing**: Monthly rental ("per bulan") is standard for villas and kos. Yearly contracts get big discounts. Always negotiate.
- **Pembantu/ART**: Many households have a "pembantu" (helper) or "ART" (asisten rumah tangga) for cleaning, laundry, cooking. This is normal and expected, not a luxury.
- **Wet and dry seasons**: Indonesia has two seasons: musim hujan (rainy, Oct-Mar) and musim kemarau/kering (dry, Apr-Sep). No spring/summer/autumn/winter.
- **Air panas culture**: Indonesians often shower with cold water ("air dingin"). Hot water ("air panas") in bathrooms is not guaranteed outside hotels.

2. **Comparatives**: lebih + adjective + dari = "more [adj] than." "Dia lebih tinggi dari saya" = She is taller than me.
3. **Superlatives**: paling + adjective = "most [adj]." "Dia paling pintar" = She is the smartest. (Also ter- + adjective in Unit 8.)
4. **Inclusive vs. exclusive "we"**: kita (includes listener) vs. kami (excludes listener). "Kita pergi?" = Shall we (all) go? "Kami dari Australia" = We (not you) are from Australia.
5. **punya (to have)**: "Saya punya dua anak" = I have two children. "Dia tidak punya mobil" = He doesn't have a car.

### Affix Notes — Unit 6

- **me-**: PRODUCTIVE. Introduce the full allomorphy table (simplified):
  - Root starts with l, m, n, r, w, y -> me- (melihat, memang, menulis, merasa, mewakili)
  - Root starts with b, f, p (p drops) -> mem- (membuat, memukul)
  - Root starts with t (t drops), d, c, j -> men- (menulis, mendapat, mencari, menjadi)
  - Root starts with s (s drops) -> meny- (menyuka -> menyukai)
  - Root starts with k (k drops), g, h, vowels -> meng- (mengambil, menggambar, menghitung, mengajar)
- **per-** in "perkenalan" (introduction): Receptive. per- + kenal + -an = the act of getting to know.
- **me-** vs. base form: In casual speech, the me- prefix is often dropped. "Cari" instead of "mencari." Both are understood. Formal writing requires me-.

### Register Notes — Unit 6

- **Family terms as pronouns**: Indonesians often call non-relatives "Mas" (older brother), "Mbak" (older sister), "Om" (uncle), "Tante" (aunt). These replace "kamu/Anda" in many contexts.
- **"Dia" is gender-neutral**: No he/she distinction. Specify with "dia laki-laki" or "dia perempuan" if needed.
- **"Sudah menikah?"**: Asking if someone is married is common small talk, not considered intrusive.

### Cultural Notes — Unit 6

- **Family is central**: Indonesian culture is very family-oriented. Expect questions about your family: "Sudah menikah?" (Married?), "Punya anak?" (Have children?), "Orang tua di mana?" (Where are your parents?).
- **Age and hierarchy**: Older people are addressed with "Pak" (sir) or "Bu" (ma'am). Age matters in social interactions. The word "kakak" (older sibling) implies respect.
- **Communal living**: Extended families often live together or very nearby. "Keluarga besar" (big family) is the norm.
- **Gotong royong**: The concept of communal helping. Neighbors help each other with events, ceremonies, and daily life.

### Grammar Points — Unit 7

1. **di- prefix (passive voice)**: PRODUCTIVE RULE. di- + root = passive verb ("is/was [verb]-ed"). The focus shifts to the thing being acted upon, not the actor.
   - periksa (examine) -> diperiksa (is examined)
   - minum (drink) -> diminum (is drunk/taken)
   - makan (eat) -> dimakan (is eaten)
   - buat (make) -> dibuat (is made)
2. **Passive usage in Indonesian**: Much more common than in English. "Obat ini diminum tiga kali sehari" = This medicine is taken three times daily. Indonesian prefers passive constructions in many everyday contexts.
3. **-kan suffix (causative/benefactive)**: RECEPTIVE. Makes verbs that act on behalf of someone or cause something.
   - berikan = give (to someone) — beri + -kan
   - tunjukkan = show (to someone) — tunjuk + -kan
   - ceritakan = tell/narrate — cerita + -kan
4. **Time conjunctions**: sebelum (before), sesudah/setelah (after). "Minum obat sebelum makan" = Take medicine before eating.
5. **Imperative mood**: Direct commands use the base verb. "Duduk!" (Sit!), "Tunggu!" (Wait!), "Berhenti!" (Stop!). Adding "tolong" makes it polite: "Tolong duduk" (Please sit down).

### Affix Notes — Unit 7

- **di-**: PRODUCTIVE. Teach as the passive counterpart to me-. If me- + verb = active ("Dokter memeriksa saya" = The doctor examines me), then di- + verb = passive ("Saya diperiksa dokter" = I am examined by the doctor). Indonesian uses passive far more than English.
- **-kan**: RECEPTIVE. Introduced in imperative forms. "Berikan" (give it), "tunjukkan" (show it), "ceritakan" (tell about it). The -kan makes the verb act on or for someone.
- **pe-** in "pencuri" (thief): pe- + curi (steal) = "one who steals." Preview of agent noun prefix (Unit 9).
- **ke-...-an** in "keadaan" (situation/state): ke- + ada + -an. Preview of abstract noun circumfix (Unit 10).

### Register Notes — Unit 7

- **Medical Indonesian is formal**: Doctors use bahasa baku. Patients should too. Use "saya" not "aku."
- **"Mau BAB"**: Indonesians euphemistically say "mau ke belakang" (want to go to the back) for needing the toilet, or use the abbreviation "BAB" (buang air besar) for medical contexts.

### Cultural Notes — Unit 7

- **Self-medication culture**: Indonesians frequently buy medicine directly from the apotek without a prescription. Pharmacists can recommend treatments.
- **Traditional medicine**: "Jamu" is traditional herbal medicine, still widely used alongside modern medicine. "Minum jamu" = drink herbal medicine.
- **Emergency numbers**: 112 (general emergency), 118/119 (ambulance). But in practice, going directly to the nearest hospital is often faster.
- **Bali expat healthcare**: International clinics (BIMC, Siloam) have English-speaking staff, but local clinics ("puskesmas") are cheaper and Indonesian language skills are essential there.

### Grammar Points — Unit 8

1. **ter- prefix (superlatives)**: PRODUCTIVE. ter- + adjective = "the most [adj]" or "-est." "Terbaik" = the best. "Tercepat" = the fastest. "Termurah" = the cheapest. Alternative to "paling + adj."
2. **ter- prefix (accidental/involuntary)**: ter- + verb = unintentional action. "Tertidur" = fell asleep (accidentally). "Terjatuh" = fell down. "Tertawa" = burst out laughing.
3. **-an suffix (nominalization)**: PRODUCTIVE. verb/adjective + -an = noun. "Makan" (eat) -> "makanan" (food). "Minum" (drink) -> "minuman" (beverage). "Main" (play) -> "mainan" (toy).
4. **akan + verb (future)**: "Saya akan pergi besok" = I will go tomorrow. "Dia akan datang nanti" = He/she will come later.
5. **ayo/mari (let's)**: "Ayo pergi!" = Let's go! "Mari kita makan" = Let us eat. "Ayo" is informal, "mari" is more formal.

### Affix Notes — Unit 8

- **ter-**: PRODUCTIVE. Two meanings:
  1. Superlative: ter- + adjective = "the most" (terbaik, termurah, tercepat)
  2. Accidental/involuntary: ter- + verb = unintentional (tertidur, terjatuh, tertawa)
- **-an**: PRODUCTIVE. Creates nouns from verbs/adjectives:
  - makan -> makanan (food)
  - minum -> minuman (drink/beverage)
  - main -> mainan (toy)
  - baca -> bacaan (reading material)
  - pekerjaan (work/job) = pe- + kerja + -an (compound affix preview)
- **per-...-an** in "pertunjukan" (performance): per- + tunjuk + -an. Receptive. Compound circumfix creating abstract/event nouns.
- **pe-...-an** in "pemandangan" (scenery): pe- + mandang + -an. Receptive preview of compound affix.

### Register Notes — Unit 8

- **Ayo vs. Mari**: "Ayo" is casual and enthusiastic. "Mari" is polite/formal. In formal speech: "Mari kita mulai" (Let us begin). In casual speech: "Ayo kita pergi!" (Let's go!).
- **Nonton vs. Menonton**: In casual speech, me- is dropped. "Nonton film" (watch a movie) instead of "menonton film." Both are correct.

### Cultural Notes — Unit 8

- **Surfing culture**: Bali is a world-famous surf destination. Kuta, Uluwatu, Canggu — knowing surf vocabulary is useful for expats.
- **Balinese temples**: "Pura" is specifically Balinese Hindu. "Masjid" (mosque) and "gereja" (church) are also common. Visitors must wear a sarong at temples.
- **Weekend activities**: Many Indonesians spend weekends at malls. "Jalan-jalan ke mall" (stroll around the mall) is a primary leisure activity.
- **Gotong royong in leisure**: Group activities are preferred over solo ones. Indonesians rarely do things alone — always invite friends.

3. **pe-...-an circumfix (process/abstract nouns)**: PRODUCTIVE.
   - didik (educate) -> pendidikan (education)
   - alam (nature/experience) -> pengalaman (experience)
   - dapat (get/obtain) -> pendapat (opinion)
   - usaha (effort) -> perusahaan (company)
4. **Formal telephone language**: "Bisa bicara dengan...?" (May I speak with...?). "Dari siapa?" (Who is calling? / From whom?). "Tolong sampaikan..." (Please convey...).

### Affix Notes — Unit 9

- **-kan**: PRODUCTIVE. Teach as "do the action for someone" or "cause the action":
  - kirimkan (send for someone)
  - sampaikan (convey/deliver)
  - selesaikan (finish/complete)
  - bagikan (share/distribute)
  - ceritakan (tell/narrate)
- **pe-**: PRODUCTIVE. Agent noun prefix (same allomorphy as me-):
  - pekerja (worker), pelajar (student), pengusaha (entrepreneur)
- **pe-...-an**: PRODUCTIVE. Process/abstract/institution noun circumfix:
  - pendidikan (education), pengalaman (experience), perusahaan (company)
- **per-...-an** in "perusahaan": per- + usaha + -an = company/enterprise. Note overlap with pe-...-an.
- **-i** in "perbaiki": Suffix -i indicates the action is directed at something. "Perbaiki" = fix/repair (directed at the object). This is a preview — full -i treatment is beyond A2.

### Register Notes — Unit 9

- **Office Indonesian is formal**: Use saya/Anda, full me- prefix, complete sentences.
- **WhatsApp culture**: Indonesians heavily use WhatsApp for work communication. "WA" (pronounced "wah-ah") is used as a verb: "Saya WA Anda nanti" = "I'll WhatsApp you later."
- **"Pak" and "Bu" in the workplace**: Always address superiors as "Pak [name]" or "Bu [name]." Never first name only with bosses.

### Cultural Notes — Unit 9

- **Digital nomad culture**: Bali (especially Canggu, Ubud) has a large digital nomad community. Co-working spaces are everywhere. Knowing work vocabulary helps with visa applications (KITAS/KITAP).
- **Indonesian work culture**: Hierarchical. Decisions flow top-down. Punctuality is flexible ("jam karet" = rubber time). Relationships matter more than deadlines.
- **Business cards**: Still common. Receive with both hands, examine briefly, never write on someone's card in front of them.

### Grammar Points — Unit 10

1. **ke-...-an circumfix (abstract nouns)**: PRODUCTIVE. Creates abstract qualities and states.
   - baik (good) -> kebaikan (goodness/kindness)
   - sehat (healthy) -> kesehatan (health)
   - budaya (culture) -> kebudayaan (culture, abstract concept)
   - indah (beautiful) -> keindahan (beauty)
2. **me-...-kan compound affix (transitive causative)**: PRODUCTIVE. Makes transitive verbs meaning "to cause/do [action] to something."
   - raya (great) -> merayakan (to celebrate)
   - jelas (clear) -> menjelaskan (to explain, lit. "to make clear")
   - cerita (story) -> menceritakan (to tell a story)
   - ingat (remember) -> mengingatkan (to remind)
3. **Conditional — kalau/jika**: "Kalau hujan, saya tidak pergi" = If it rains, I won't go. "Jika" is more formal. Placed at the start or middle of sentence.
4. **Expressing opinions**: "Menurut saya..." (According to me / In my opinion...). "Saya pikir..." (I think...). "Saya rasa..." (I feel...).
5. **Agreeing/disagreeing**: "Saya setuju" (I agree). "Saya tidak setuju" (I disagree). "Mungkin" (Maybe). "Benar" (That's correct). "Saya kurang setuju" (I somewhat disagree — polite form).

### Affix Notes — Unit 10

- **ke-...-an**: PRODUCTIVE. Abstract/state noun circumfix:
  - kebaikan (goodness), kesehatan (health), kebudayaan (culture), keindahan (beauty), keamanan (safety)
  - Pattern: ke- + root adjective/noun + -an = abstract quality/state
- **me-...-kan**: PRODUCTIVE. Transitive causative compound affix:
  - merayakan (celebrate), menjelaskan (explain), menceritakan (narrate)
  - Pattern: me- + root + -kan = "to cause [root] to happen" or "to do [root] to something"
  - Same me- allomorphy applies (mem-, men-, meny-, meng-)
- **ber-** continues: berterima kasih (to be grateful), bersyukur (to be thankful)
- **Full affix mastery review**: By end of Unit 10, learners have encountered all major Indonesian affixes: me-, di-, ber-, ter-, pe-, se-, -kan, -an, -nya, -i, ke-...-an, pe-...-an, per-...-an, me-...-kan

### Register Notes — Unit 10

- **Formal opinion language**: Meetings and discussions use formal register. "Menurut pendapat saya..." (In my opinion...). "Saya berpendapat bahwa..." (I am of the opinion that...).
- **Polite disagreement**: Direct "tidak" (no) is considered rude in many contexts. Use softeners: "Mungkin bisa dilihat dari sisi lain" (Maybe we can look from another angle). "Saya kurang setuju" (I somewhat disagree) instead of "Saya tidak setuju."
- **Religious greetings**: "Assalamualaikum" (Muslim), "Om Swastiastu" (Balinese Hindu), "Shalom" (Christian) — learners should recognize these.

### Cultural Notes — Unit 10

- **Pancasila**: Five principles of Indonesian national philosophy. Understanding these helps understand Indonesian values: belief in God, humanity, unity, democracy, social justice.
- **Major holidays**: Idul Fitri/Lebaran (end of Ramadan), Nyepi (Balinese Day of Silence), Christmas, Galungan/Kuningan (Balinese Hindu), Independence Day (August 17).
- **Nyepi**: Unique to Bali — the entire island shuts down for 24 hours. No lights, no travel, no noise. Even the airport closes. Foreigners must stay indoors.
- **Gotong royong (repeated)**: Community spirit is the deepest cultural value. Helping neighbors, communal work, shared responsibility.
- **"Bule" and being foreign**: "Bule" refers to foreigners (especially white Westerners). Not pejorative — just descriptive. Used casually and openly.
- **Saying goodbye**: Indonesians have two farewells: "Selamat tinggal" (said by the one leaving, lit. "safe staying") and "Selamat jalan" (said by the one staying, lit. "safe traveling").
